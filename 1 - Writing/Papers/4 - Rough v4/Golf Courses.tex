\begin{abstract}
\noindent This thesis quantifies the aggregate opportunity cost of recreational land in the United States by applying the appraisal principle of Highest and Best Use (HBU) to a national dataset of 16,297 golf courses, with a Honolulu County validation study. A tri-language pipeline (Python, R, Julia) integrates Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA) and United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) county-level land valuations with OpenStreetMap-derived acreage, resolving 28.8\% missing acreage through Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE; $M = 100$ per language) pooled via Rubin's Rules. The pooled aggregate opportunity cost is approximately \textbf{\$944 billion}, with an observed-only floor of \textbf{\$788 billion} computed without imputation. Spatial concentration is extreme: California, Florida, New York, Texas, and Hawaii together account for over half of the national total, and a pooled logarithmic decomposition shows urban courses valued at approximately 60$\times$ rural courses on a per-acre basis, robust across all three implementations.

\noindent The Honolulu County validation integrates this national model with parcel-level cadastral and zoning data. A pilot comparison of six high-profile Hawaii courses against official tax assessments yields a model-to-assessed ratio of \textbf{1.33$\times$}, narrowing in urban Honolulu (1.16--1.23$\times$) and widening in rural Big Island courses (1.49--1.69$\times$). A full pipeline analysis identifies 1,072 unique Tax Map Key parcels comprising Oahu's golf footprint with a pooled opportunity cost of \$28.6 billion. Crucially, \textbf{81.7\% of this acreage sits within Preservation or Federal/Military zones}, where residential redevelopment is legally prohibited; golf occupies 25.4\% of all Resort-zoned land on Oahu and concentrates 63.2\% of its parcels in the Ewa district, the island's primary suburban housing development corridor.

\noindent The macroeconomic opportunity cost of golf course land is large, geographically concentrated, and empirically tractable, but its realization is bounded by a layered regulatory architecture the HBU framework alone cannot dissolve.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The allocation and regulation of urban and suburban land represent critical challenges within modern spatial economics. In many major United States metropolitan areas, the supply of developable land is highly inelastic, driven largely by restrictive land-use regulations rather than physical geographic constraints \parencite{Glaeser2017, Gyourko2021}. This artificial scarcity has produced a pronounced divergence between the physical cost of construction and the market price of housing, generating a substantial regulatory ``zoning tax'' that inflates residential land values \parencite{Glaeser2005, Davis2021}.

Amidst this intense competition for spatial resources, the American golf course represents a massive and highly specific land-use typology. Historically, real estate developers utilized golf courses as ``surrogate public goods'' to cross-subsidize the construction of master-planned communities, capturing exorbitant price premiums on adjacent residential lots \parencite{Limehouse2012}. Empirical evidence suggests, however, that the marginal amenity value of proximity to golf courses has depreciated over time as consumer preferences shift toward natural open spaces \parencite{Cho2009}. Despite this structural decline in recreational demand and the escalating value of residential land, hundreds of thousands of acres of prime real estate remain locked in this single-use capacity \parencite{Weinand2025}.

\subsection{Motivation: The View from Honolulu}

This study originated in Honolulu, where the housing pressures described in the national literature are not abstractions. As a graduate student at the University of Hawai`i at M\=anoa, I encountered a state with one of the nation's most acute housing shortages alongside a striking density of golf facilities --- 33 distinct courses on the island of Oahu alone, occupying over 6,000 acres of zoned and developable land. The City and County of Honolulu maintains comprehensive parcel-level cadastral and zoning data at the Tax Map Key (TMK) resolution, providing an unusual opportunity to test whether the national-scale opportunity cost framework holds up against ground-truth municipal records.

This local context shaped the design of the project in two ways. First, it suggested that a purely national aggregate, however carefully constructed, risks being dismissed as a theoretical exercise. A defensible empirical claim about the value of golf course land requires anchoring against the assessed values that municipalities actually use to tax these parcels. Second, it suggested that Hawaii itself --- with high-value urban courses, rural courses on the neighbor islands, and a regulatory landscape dominated by Preservation and Federal/Military zoning --- is a useful microcosm for the broader American pattern. The two scales are complementary: the national dataset establishes the magnitude and geographic structure of the spatial misallocation, while the Honolulu micro-study tests whether that magnitude survives contact with cadastral reality.

\subsection{Research Framework}

To quantify the macroeconomic inefficiency of recreational land allocation, this thesis applies the foundational real estate appraisal principle of Highest and Best Use (HBU). HBU dictates that land should be valued based on its most profitable, legally permissible, and physically possible use \parencite{AppraisalInstitute2020}. By treating the existing golf course as the baseline, a counterfactual valuation model can establish the aggregate opportunity cost ($V_{OC}$) of this land. The core economic framework is defined by the following equation:

\begin{equation}
    V_{OC} = \max(V_{Res}, V_{Ag}) - V_{Current}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $V_{Res}$ represents the land value under a residential development counterfactual, $V_{Ag}$ the value under an agricultural counterfactual, and $V_{Current}$ the capitalized value of the land in its current recreational use.

In a frictionless, perfectly competitive market, land would naturally transition to the use that maximizes its residual value. The transition of golf course land to its HBU is severely impeded, however, by a dual layer of institutional frictions. Publicly, municipal zoning ordinances frequently prohibit high-density redevelopment. Privately, properties are often encumbered by restrictive covenants and Homeowner Association (HOA) agreements that legally mandate the preservation of the golf course in perpetuity, regardless of its financial feasibility \parencite{Ellickson2022, Korngold2024}. To isolate the pure economic value of the land, this thesis adopts a formal \textit{Hypothetical Condition} \parencite{AppraisalInstitute2020} that suspends these legal frictions for analytical purposes. The Honolulu micro-study then re-introduces zoning as a binding constraint, yielding a \textit{legally-permissible HBU} that bounds the unrestricted estimate.

\subsection{Research Questions}

This thesis addresses two coupled research questions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textit{What is the aggregate financial opportunity cost of U.S. golf courses when evaluated against their Highest and Best Use counterfactuals of residential or agricultural development, and how is that cost distributed spatially?}
    \item \textit{When the national HBU framework is validated against parcel-level cadastral and zoning data in Honolulu County, how closely do the model's estimates align with municipal tax assessments, and what share of the estimated opportunity cost is bounded by current zoning?}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Contributions}

This thesis makes three contributions to the literature on land use, housing supply, and regulatory economics. First, it constructs and publishes a national-scale spatial dataset of 16,297 golf courses with imputed acreage and county-anchored land valuations, implemented in three independent computational stacks (Python, R, and Julia) for cross-language replicability. Second, it produces what is, to my knowledge, the first parcel-level validation of an HBU-based national opportunity cost model against municipal tax assessment data, anchored in 1,072 unique TMK parcels from the City and County of Honolulu. Third, it documents a regulatory paradox specific to high-value coastal markets: the bulk of golf course land in Honolulu County sits in Preservation or Federal/Military zones, meaning the very locations where opportunity cost is highest are also those where statutory redevelopment is most restricted.

\subsection{Roadmap}

The remainder of this thesis is organized as follows. \textbf{Section 2} reviews the relevant economic and legal literature, establishing the macroeconomic context of regulatory land scarcity, the microeconomic pricing of recreational amenities, the institutional frictions that govern land-use transitions, and the property tax assessment literature that informs the Hawaii validation. \textbf{Section 3} formalizes the theoretical framework, translating HBU into a neoclassical spatial model and introducing the legally-permissible HBU concept used in the micro-study. \textbf{Section 4} details the two-scale research design, including the national pipeline (Phases 1--4) and the Honolulu County pipeline (Phase 5). \textbf{Section 5} presents the empirical results across both scales, with the Hawaii validation as the empirical anchor. \textbf{Section 6} discusses the macroeconomic and policy implications, including the failure of Coasian bargaining, the validation gap, the zoning-bounded HBU, and methodological limitations. \textbf{Section 7} concludes with a summary of findings and directions for future economic research.

\section{Literature Review}

This thesis bridges four strands of economic and legal literature: the macroeconomic valuation of urban land and regulatory scarcity, the microeconomic pricing of recreational amenities, the institutional frictions that govern land-use transitions, and the property tax assessment literature that informs the Hawaii validation study. By synthesizing these domains, this research establishes the theoretical foundation necessary to quantify the aggregate opportunity cost of golf course land at both the national and county scales.

\subsection{Urban Land Valuation and Regulatory Scarcity}

The primary driver of the opportunity cost associated with underutilized urban land is the escalating value of residential real estate ($V_{Res}$). Foundational work by \textcite{Davis2008} decomposes housing prices into structure costs and land values, demonstrating that the vast heterogeneity in U.S. housing prices is driven almost entirely by the price of the underlying land, rather than physical construction costs. \textcite{Davis2021} extend this analysis by providing highly granular, tract-level estimates of residential land prices, confirming that land values follow steep spatial gradients that peak sharply near central business districts. The Davis et al. county-level dataset, distributed by the FHFA, forms the basis of the urban valuation proxy used in this study.

This extreme appreciation in land value is largely attributed to artificial supply constraints. \textcite{Glaeser2005} introduce the concept of the ``zoning tax,'' defined as the gap between the market price of a housing unit and its marginal cost of production. In a frictionless market, free entry ensures that prices equal average costs; restrictive land-use regulations sever this relationship, bidding up the extensive margin value of land \parencite{Gyourko2021}. Using micro-data on vacant land transactions, \textcite{Gyourko2021} estimate that zoning taxes in San Francisco, Los Angeles, and Seattle exceed typical household incomes everywhere within those metropolitan areas --- a result that underscores the extensive margin distortion created by binding regulatory constraints.

The macroeconomic implications of inelastic housing supply are severe. \textcite{Glaeser2017} argue that regulatory constraints in high-productivity markets prevent the efficient spatial allocation of labor, resulting in significant deadweight loss to aggregate national output. Consequently, the opportunity cost of maintaining large tracts of single-use recreational land in these constrained markets is not merely a localized inefficiency but a macroeconomic distortion. \textcite{Cheshire2002} reinforce this point with a UK-based welfare analysis showing that restrictive land-use planning operates as a regressive tax on housing affordability. The empirical regularity across this literature is consistent: where housing supply is constrained by regulation, the marginal value of converting non-residential land to housing is large, and the welfare cost of failing to do so is borne disproportionately by lower-income households.

\subsection{The Microeconomics of Recreational Amenities}

To accurately model the current value of golf courses ($V_{Current}$), it is necessary to understand the economic mechanisms that generate their revenue. Building on the two-stage hedonic pricing model established by \textcite{Rosen1974}, \textcite{Limehouse2010} demonstrate that environmental quality and recreational exclusivity are credence goods capitalized directly into golf course pricing.

The primary economic driver of golf course development, however, is often not the recreational operation itself but the ``joint production'' of the course and adjacent real estate \parencite{Limehouse2012}. Developers utilize the golf course as a surrogate public good to cross-subsidize the project, extracting price premiums from the surrounding residential lots. While this model historically maximized developer profit, longitudinal hedonic analyses indicate that the amenity value of golf courses is subject to structural depreciation. \textcite{Cho2009} find that while proximity to open space acts as a substitute for private lot size, consumer preferences have shifted significantly over time: the marginal value of proximity to natural amenities such as public parks and water bodies has increased, whereas the premium associated specifically with golf courses has declined measurably. \textcite{Weinand2025} push the spatial inefficiency argument further, noting that the global land footprint of golf courses vastly exceeds the area required for utility-scale renewable energy infrastructure --- introducing alternative, non-residential counterfactuals for land optimization.

\subsection{Institutional Frictions and Land Use Rigidity}

If the residential value of land ($V_{Res}$) exceeds its current recreational value ($V_{Current}$), standard neoclassical theory dictates that the land should be redeveloped. The failure of the market to execute this transition is explained by severe institutional frictions and transaction costs that prevent fluid Coasian bargaining \parencite{Coase1960}. Importantly, the Coasian framework does not predict that any particular allocation is efficient under positive transaction costs; it predicts that the initial assignment of property rights determines the resulting allocation. The current allocation grants veto power to incumbent homeowners and golf course members, which is what produces the persistence of the existing use.

The rigidity of current land use is enforced by a dual layer of public and private regulation. Publicly, municipal zoning laws frequently prohibit the densification required to achieve Highest and Best Use \parencite{Fischel2015}. While these planning policies generate localized environmental amenities, the net welfare effect of restrictive regulatory regimes is substantially negative \parencite{Cheshire2002}.

Privately, land-use transitions are blocked by restrictive covenants. \textcite{Ellickson2022} documents that the majority of modern housing developments are tethered by covenants that mandate the preservation of existing land uses --- including, frequently, golf courses --- in perpetuity. Because these covenants typically require supermajority votes to amend, they suffer from severe collective action problems and status quo bias, effectively freezing the land use even when the underlying economic case for redevelopment is overwhelming. \textcite{Korngold2024} argues that legislative zoning reform is insufficient unless parallel private covenants are simultaneously voided under public policy doctrines, but cautions that such voiding may be classified as a regulatory taking under Fifth Amendment doctrine, requiring just compensation to incumbent beneficiaries. This literature confirms that the legal barriers to redevelopment are formidable, justifying the use of a Hypothetical Condition in the national valuation while motivating the binding-zoning analysis applied in the Hawaii micro-study.

\subsection{Property Tax Assessment and Market Value}

The Honolulu validation study compares the model's HBU-based opportunity cost estimates against official tax assessment values published by the City and County of Honolulu. A substantial property tax literature documents that assessed values systematically diverge from market values, particularly for properties whose current use does not represent their highest and best use \parencite{AppraisalInstitute2020}. The Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice distinguish between the value of property in its current use (relevant for ad valorem taxation) and its market value at HBU (relevant for transactions and economic analysis), with the latter typically exceeding the former when current use is suboptimal.

This distinction is foundational to the validation logic of the present study. A model-to-assessed ratio greater than unity is not evidence that the model is wrong; it is evidence that the model is measuring something different from what assessors are measuring. The model estimates value under a hypothetical residential or agricultural redeployment, while assessors estimate value under the current recreational use, often with statutory protections for use-classified properties. The magnitude and structure of the divergence between these two value concepts is itself an economically meaningful object of study, and one that this thesis attempts to characterize empirically using the Hawaii dataset.

\subsection{Synthesis and Contribution}

While existing literature extensively documents the regulatory inflation of residential land values and the microeconomic pricing of golf course amenities, there is a distinct lack of empirical research quantifying the aggregate spatial intersection of these two phenomena, and almost no work that validates national-scale HBU models against parcel-level municipal assessment data. This thesis contributes to the literature in three ways. First, it applies rigorous real estate appraisal frameworks \parencite{AppraisalInstitute2020} to a granular national spatial dataset, calculating the aggregate opportunity cost of maintaining recreational assets in supply-constrained housing markets. Second, it integrates parcel-level cadastral and zoning data from the City and County of Honolulu to test the model's external validity against ground-truth municipal records. Third, it documents the regulatory architecture --- specifically the dominance of Preservation and Federal/Military zoning --- that bounds the realizability of the unrestricted opportunity cost in at least one high-value coastal market.

\section{Theoretical Framework}

This section establishes the formal economic model used to quantify the spatial misallocation of recreational land. The framework integrates neoclassical urban economics with the professional appraisal doctrine of Highest and Best Use (HBU) to derive a mathematical function for aggregate opportunity cost, then introduces a legally-permissible variant that bounds the unrestricted estimate by binding zoning constraints.

\subsection{Neoclassical Land Allocation and Highest and Best Use}

In a frictionless, perfectly competitive spatial market, land is allocated to the use that generates the maximum residual value. This principle aligns with the appraisal standard of Highest and Best Use (HBU), defined as the reasonably probable and legal use of vacant land or an improved property that is physically possible, appropriately supported, financially feasible, and results in the highest value \parencite{AppraisalInstitute2020}.

For a given parcel of land $i$, let $V_{i,k}$ represent the present value of the land under use $k$, where $k \in \{1, 2, ..., K\}$ represents the set of all physically possible and financially feasible uses. Under conditions of spatial equilibrium, the market value of the parcel, $V_i^*$, is determined by the use that maximizes this value:

\begin{equation}
    V_i^* = \max \{V_{i,1}, V_{i,2}, ..., V_{i,K}\}
\end{equation}

When the current use of the land, $V_{Current}$, diverges from $V_i^*$, an economic inefficiency exists. The magnitude of this inefficiency is the opportunity cost.

\subsection{The Opportunity Cost Function}

To quantify the inefficiency of land currently allocated to golf courses, this analysis restricts the set of counterfactual uses $K$ to the two most probable alternative land uses based on spatial geography: residential development ($V_{Res}$) and agricultural production ($V_{Ag}$). The fundamental opportunity cost ($V_{OC}$) for a specific golf course parcel $i$ is defined as the difference between its value under its HBU counterfactual and its value in its current recreational state:

\begin{equation}
    V_{OC, i} = \max(V_{Res, i}, V_{Ag, i}) - V_{Current, i}
\end{equation}

The aggregate national opportunity cost is the summation of $V_{OC, i}$ across all $N$ golf courses in the dataset:

\begin{equation}
    \text{Aggregate } V_{OC} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[ \max(V_{Res, i}, V_{Ag, i}) - V_{Current, i} \right]
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Operational Form: Gross HBU Counterfactual}

In the empirical national-scale implementation, $V_{Current}$ is not directly observed for each of the 16,297 courses; the national-scale data sources used in this study (FHFA county-level residential land prices and USDA county-level agricultural land values) characterize $V_{Res}$ and $V_{Ag}$ but do not provide course-level capitalized recreational income. The national-scale empirical estimand is therefore the \textit{gross HBU counterfactual}:

\begin{equation}
    V_{OC, i}^{\text{gross}} = \max(V_{Res, i}, V_{Ag, i})
\end{equation}

\noindent which represents the upper bound on the per-parcel opportunity cost. The Hawaii micro-study (Section 4.3 and Section 5.4) addresses this limitation directly by anchoring the gross estimate against official tax-assessed values for a subset of Honolulu County courses, producing an empirically estimated model-to-assessed ratio that characterizes the relationship between $V_{OC}^{\text{gross}}$ and a defensible proxy for $V_{Current}$.

\subsubsection{Deconstructing the Counterfactuals}

The variables within the core equation are defined by distinct microeconomic determinants:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item $\mathbf{V_{Res}}$ \textbf{(Residential Value):} The extensive margin value of the land if subdivided and developed for single-family or multi-family housing. In highly regulated metropolitan statistical areas, $V_{Res}$ is artificially inflated by a regulatory zoning tax ($\tau_z$), which represents the scarcity premium induced by supply-side restrictions \parencite{Glaeser2005, Gyourko2021}. Therefore, $V_{Res}$ is a function of organic housing demand ($D_h$), physical construction costs ($C_c$), and regulatory scarcity ($\tau_z$).
    \item $\mathbf{V_{Ag}}$ \textbf{(Agricultural Value):} The capitalized value of the land's agricultural yield, primarily applicable to courses located in rural or exurban geographies where residential demand is insufficient to justify development costs.
    \item $\mathbf{V_{Current}}$ \textbf{(Current Recreational Value):} The capitalized net operating income of the golf course. This value depends heavily on the joint production model in which the course serves as a surrogate public good that generates price premiums for adjacent real estate \parencite{Limehouse2012}, and on operating fundamentals (rounds played, membership fees, food and beverage revenue) that vary substantially across facilities. In the Hawaii validation study, official municipal tax assessments serve as a defensible proxy for $V_{Current}$, since assessors value these properties under their current recreational use.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Legally-Permissible HBU}

The HBU framework as stated assumes the land is legally available for redevelopment to its most productive use. In practice, this is rarely the case. Standard appraisal practice distinguishes between the unrestricted maximum-value use and the use that is legally permissible under current zoning, environmental, and contractual restrictions \parencite{AppraisalInstitute2020}. To formalize this distinction, let $L_i \subseteq K$ denote the subset of uses that are legally permissible for parcel $i$ under current zoning. The legally-permissible HBU value is then:

\begin{equation}
    V_{i, \text{legal}}^* = \max\{V_{i,k} : k \in L_i\}
\end{equation}

\noindent and the corresponding legally-permissible opportunity cost is:

\begin{equation}
    V_{OC, i}^{\text{legal}} = V_{i, \text{legal}}^* - V_{Current, i}
\end{equation}

By construction, $V_{OC, i}^{\text{legal}} \leq V_{OC, i}^{\text{gross}}$, with equality only when current zoning permits the unrestricted HBU. In the Hawaii micro-study, $L_i$ is operationalized using the Honolulu County zoning layer: parcels classified as Preservation (P-1, P-2) or Federal/Military (F-1) cannot legally support residential redevelopment, so for these parcels $V_{Res}$ is excluded from the feasible set. This produces a zoning-bounded estimate of the realizable opportunity cost --- one that quantifies how much of the unrestricted HBU value is rendered theoretical by current land-use law.

\subsection{Institutional Frictions and Market Failure}

If $V_{OC, i} > 0$, rational economic actors should execute a transaction to redevelop the land. The persistence of golf courses in high-value urban areas indicates a market failure driven by severe institutional frictions ($F$). These frictions consist of public regulatory constraints ($Z$), such as municipal zoning ordinances that prohibit high-density development, and private contractual constraints ($C$), such as restrictive covenants enforced by Homeowner Associations \parencite{Ellickson2022, Korngold2024}. Mathematically, redevelopment occurs only if the opportunity cost exceeds the total institutional friction cost:

\begin{equation}
    V_{OC, i} > F(Z_i, C_i)
\end{equation}

The Coasian framework is instructive here, but in a more limited way than is sometimes claimed \parencite{Coase1960}. With zero transaction costs, the initial allocation of rights does not affect the efficient outcome. With positive transaction costs --- the empirically relevant case --- the initial allocation determines the equilibrium allocation. The current legal regime grants veto power over redevelopment to incumbent homeowners and golf course members through zoning and covenants, with the result that even when $V_{OC}$ is large, $F$ can be larger still. Voiding these veto rights, as proposed by \textcite{Korngold2024}, would shift the equilibrium allocation by reassigning rights, but this shift is itself politically and constitutionally contested.

To isolate the pure economic value of the land --- distinct from the regulatory architecture that currently governs its use --- this thesis applies the appraisal doctrine of a \textit{Hypothetical Condition} \parencite{AppraisalInstitute2020} at the national scale, setting $F(Z_i, C_i) = 0$. The Honolulu micro-study then re-introduces zoning as a binding constraint via the legally-permissible HBU framework defined above. The two estimates --- unrestricted and zoning-bounded --- are complementary: the first measures the economic potential of the land if institutional frictions were dissolved, and the second measures the share of that potential that current law permits to be realized.

\section{Data and Methodology}

To answer the research questions posed in Section 1.3, this thesis adopts a two-scale empirical research design. The national-scale analysis (Section 4.2) constructs a 16,297-course dataset and produces the aggregate opportunity cost estimate. The Honolulu County micro-study (Section 4.3) integrates parcel-level cadastral and zoning data to test the national model against ground-truth municipal records. The two scales are not independent: the Hawaii pipeline reuses the national-scale outputs as inputs, and the validation logic depends on both pipelines producing consistent estimates for the Hawaii subset of the national dataset.

\subsection{Two-Scale Research Design}

The national pipeline establishes \textit{breadth} --- estimates for every county and every course in the contiguous United States plus Alaska and Hawaii, computed from federally maintained land-valuation datasets and OpenStreetMap-derived course geometries. The Honolulu pipeline establishes \textit{depth} --- parcel-level resolution for the 33 deduplicated golf courses on the island of Oahu, including assessed values, dominant zoning class, and Tax Map Key (TMK) identifiers from the City and County of Honolulu. Each scale is implemented in three independent computational stacks (Python, R, and Julia), and the analyses are pooled across both languages and Multiple Imputation runs using Rubin's Rules. Statistical estimates are reported as both per-language Rubin-pooled values and as a Grand Mean across the three languages, providing a transparent measure of cross-implementation variability.

\subsection{National Pipeline (Phases 1--4)}

\subsubsection{Dataset Scope and Sourcing}

The primary dataset consists of $N = 16{,}297$ golf course observations across the United States. Spatial point coordinates (latitude and longitude), mapped to the WGS84 coordinate reference system, were spatially joined to U.S. Census Bureau county shapefiles (2022 vintage) to assign a 5-digit Federal Information Processing Standard (FIPS) code to each course. To establish the HBU counterfactuals, two distinct 2022 land valuation datasets were integrated:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Residential Land Values ($V_{Res}$):} Sourced from the Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA) county-level panel data \parencite{Davis2021}, joined via 5-digit FIPS codes.
    \item \textbf{Agricultural Land Values ($V_{Ag}$):} Sourced from the United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) National Agricultural Statistics Service county-level survey data, joined via the same FIPS keys.
\end{enumerate}

A FIPS zero-padding bug was identified and corrected during pipeline development: county FIPS codes stored as integers lose their leading zeros (e.g., \texttt{1001} instead of \texttt{"01001"}), causing silent join mismatches with the USDA and FHFA datasets. The fix enforces 5-digit zero-padded string formatting before any join operation, and was applied identically across all three language implementations.

\subsubsection{Hybrid Valuation Algorithm}

Because land supply elasticity and the relevant HBU vary drastically across geographic submarkets \parencite{Davis2008}, applying a uniform residential counterfactual nationally would systematically overestimate opportunity costs in inelastic regions. To address this, a hybrid valuation algorithm assigns each course to either the residential or the agricultural counterfactual based on the 2023 USDA Rural-Urban Continuum Codes (RUCC):

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Urban classification (RUCC 1, 2, 3):} Courses in metropolitan counties were assigned the FHFA county-level residential price per acre as $V_{Res}$.
    \item \textbf{Rural classification (RUCC 4 through 9):} Courses in non-metropolitan counties were assigned the USDA county-level agricultural price per acre as $V_{Ag}$.
\end{itemize}

\noindent The course-level Baseline Value Per Acre is then $V_{Res}$ if the county is classified urban, $V_{Ag}$ if rural. This procedure produces a defensible per-acre proxy for 93.3\% of the courses in the dataset.

\subsubsection{Polygon Acreage Extraction}

Course acreage is not directly reported in the source dataset. To compute it, golf course boundary polygons were extracted from a national-scale OpenStreetMap (OSM) PBF dump (approximately 11~GB) using a memory-efficient streaming parser. Polygons were reprojected to EPSG:5070 (NAD83 / Conus Albers) for planar area calculation, and filtered to drop polygons below 5 acres (likely fragments or individual greens) or above 1,500 acres (likely mega-resort blobs spanning multiple distinct courses). A two-pass spatial join then matched these polygons to the course list: a primary \texttt{intersects} join, followed by a fallback \texttt{nearest} join with a 500-meter cap for courses whose listed coordinates fall outside the mapped fairway (typically because the coordinate refers to a clubhouse or parking lot). This process produced OSM-derived acreage for 71.2\% of the courses (11,605 of 16,297). For the remaining 28.8\% (4,687 courses), a fallback against the U.S. Census \texttt{tigris} ``Area Landmarks'' dataset was attempted but produced limited additional coverage; the bulk of the missing acreage was resolved through Multiple Imputation, described below.

\subsubsection{Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations}

To handle the 28.8\% of courses missing OSM-derived acreage, the analysis applies Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE), following the Bayesian framework established by \textcite{Rubin1987} and implemented per the operational guidance of \textcite{Burren2018}. For each language implementation, $M = 100$ complete datasets were generated, yielding 300 imputed datasets in total. Random Forest-based imputation methods were used (\texttt{miceforest} in Python, \texttt{mice} with \texttt{method='rf'} in R, custom implementation in Julia) to handle non-linear spatial relationships and prevent impossible negative-value predictions, a known risk with linear imputers \parencite{Burren2018}. The predictor matrix included \texttt{Holes}, \texttt{Ownership\_Type}, \texttt{county\_type}, \texttt{Longitude}, and \texttt{Latitude}.

The choice of $M = 100$ exceeds the conventional minimum of $M = 5$ recommended by \textcite{Rubin1987} by an order of magnitude. This larger value was chosen to produce stable Rubin-pooled estimates for the aggregate national valuation (a sum-of-products of imputed values) and to enable direct visualization of the convergence of the imputed acreage distribution toward the observed distribution as $M$ increases.

\subsubsection{Tri-Language Cross-Validation}

The entire pipeline --- data ingestion, FIPS join, hybrid valuation, OSM polygon extraction, MICE imputation, and aggregation --- is implemented in three independent computational stacks: Python (\texttt{geopandas}, \texttt{miceforest}, \texttt{statsmodels}), R (\texttt{sf}, \texttt{tigris}, \texttt{mice}, \texttt{lm}), and Julia (\texttt{ArchGDAL}, custom imputation, \texttt{GLM.jl}). Each stack reads identical source files and produces independently named output files (\texttt{Py\_*}, \texttt{R\_*}, \texttt{Jl\_*}). Statistical parity across the three implementations is checked at every phase, and known asymmetries (such as R's use of \texttt{final\_acreage} versus Python and Julia's use of \texttt{osm\_acreage} due to a documented Phase 2 difference in fallback behavior) are recorded explicitly. Any divergence in aggregate estimates across the three languages signals a pipeline defect rather than a substantive empirical finding, and triggers a debugging pass before the result is reported.

\subsubsection{Parameter Pooling and Rubin's Rules}

All statistical estimates are derived by performing the analysis independently across the $M = 100$ imputed datasets per language and then pooling the results using Rubin's Rules \parencite{Rubin1987, Burren2018}. The aggregate national land value is calculated by summing the total opportunity cost within each imputed dataset and then taking the arithmetic mean of those sums. For coefficient-level estimates (Section 5.3), Rubin's Rules combine within-imputation and between-imputation variance to produce pooled standard errors that correctly reflect the uncertainty introduced by imputation. The Grand Mean across the three language implementations is reported as the headline national figure, with per-language pooled estimates retained as a robustness check.

In addition to the MICE-pooled estimate, this thesis reports an \textit{observed-only} aggregate computed on the 11,605 courses with directly measured OSM polygon acreage. This figure does not depend on imputation in any way and serves as a strict lower bound on the national opportunity cost.

\subsection{Honolulu County Pipeline (Phase 5)}

The Honolulu pipeline takes the national-scale outputs and integrates them with parcel-level data published by the City and County of Honolulu. The pipeline is structured in two tracks: a manual pilot validation against tax assessments for a small set of high-profile courses (Phase 5a), and an automated full-pipeline analysis at the parcel level for all golf courses on Oahu (Phase 5b).

\subsubsection{Cadastral and Zoning Data Integration}

Three parcel-level datasets from the City and County of Honolulu are integrated with the national-scale outputs:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Honolulu Cadastral GeoPackage:} Parcel boundary geometries with Tax Map Key (TMK) identifiers covering the entire island of Oahu.
    \item \textbf{Honolulu Tax Roll CSV:} Parcel-level attributes including assessed land value, assessed building value, zoning classification, and district code.
    \item \textbf{Honolulu Zoning GeoPackage:} Polygon boundaries for all 18 zoning classifications on Oahu (residential R-3.5 through R-20, agricultural AG-1 and AG-2, preservation P-1 and P-2, federal/military F-1, resort, business, country, and others), distributed in EPSG:3760 (NAD83(HARN) Hawaii Zone 3, US survey feet).
\end{enumerate}

\noindent All datasets are reprojected to EPSG:5070 to maintain spatial consistency with the national pipeline.

The Phase 2 OSM golf course polygons for Oahu are spatially intersected with the Honolulu cadastral parcels to identify which TMKs constitute each golf course. This produces a list of 1,072 unique TMK parcels comprising the Oahu golf footprint, covering 8,564 acres of OSM-derived legal area.

\subsubsection{Phase 5a: Pilot Validation Against Tax Assessments}

The pilot validation track manually compares model HBU estimates against official tax assessment values for six high-profile Hawaii golf courses spanning all four counties (Honolulu, Maui, Hawaii, Kauai). For each course, the model estimate is the per-course opportunity cost from the national pipeline (Phase 1 baseline value per acre $\times$ Phase 2 OSM acreage, MICE-pooled where acreage was imputed), and the assessed value is the published 2022 land assessment from the relevant county tax office. The model-to-assessed ratio is computed for each course, and the ratios are compared across urban and rural settings to characterize the systematic relationship between the two value concepts.

\subsubsection{Phase 5b: Full TMK and Zoning Analysis}

The full pipeline track processes all 33 deduplicated golf courses on Oahu in six steps:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textit{OSM polygon extraction.} Restrict the Phase 2 OSM polygons to an Oahu bounding box; compute point-in-polygon match rates for the Phase 1 course list.
    \item \textit{Parcel intersection.} Spatially intersect OSM golf polygons with the Honolulu cadastre to produce a TMK list with associated golf-overlap acreage per parcel.
    \item \textit{Economic validation.} Pool $M = 100$ imputed economic datasets per language via Rubin's Rules; deduplicate spatially adjacent parcels (500-meter cap); compute the pooled Oahu aggregate opportunity cost and 95\% confidence interval.
    \item \textit{TMK diagnostic merge.} Verify that the TMK identifiers from the cadastral GeoPackage match the format used in the Honolulu tax roll CSV.
    \item \textit{Geographic concentration.} Aggregate parcel counts by Honolulu zone code (1--9) to identify district-level concentration.
    \item \textit{Zoning intersection.} Spatially intersect each golf parcel with the Honolulu zoning layer; assign each parcel its dominant zoning class via largest-overlap area; compute the share of total Oahu golf acreage in each zoning class, and the share of each zoning class occupied by golf.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Cross-language parity is again checked at each step. A small documented discrepancy in the P-1 (Restricted Preservation) intersection acreage between R's \texttt{sf::st\_intersection} (523.5 acres) and Python/Julia (744.6 acres) is attributed to edge-case geometry handling at the polygon boundary; the Python/Julia results are used as canonical for the headline figures, and the R figure is reported as a cross-check.

The output of Phase 5b is a complete characterization of the Oahu golf footprint at parcel resolution: how much land is involved, where it sits geographically, what zoning class governs it, what the aggregate opportunity cost is under the unrestricted HBU framework, and what share of that cost is bounded by current zoning under the legally-permissible HBU framework defined in Section 3.3.

\section{Empirical Results}

This section presents the quantitative findings derived from the spatial valuation model and the multiply imputed datasets. Section 5.1 reports the aggregate national opportunity cost and characterizes the stability of the MICE-pooled estimate. Section 5.2 documents the spatial concentration of value across states and counties. Section 5.3 presents the pooled logarithmic regression. Section 5.4 introduces the Hawaii validation results, which serve as the empirical anchor for the national estimate.

\subsection{Aggregate National Opportunity Cost}

Pooling the results across $M = 100$ imputed datasets per language and computing the Grand Mean across the three independent implementations, the aggregate opportunity cost ($V_{OC}^{\text{gross}}$) of the 16,297 U.S. golf courses is approximately \textbf{\$944 billion}. The cross-language spread is narrow: the Python pipeline produces \$0.943T, R produces \$0.945T, and Julia produces \$0.943T, with the Grand Mean at \$0.944T.

To establish a strict lower bound that does not depend on imputation, the same pipeline was run restricted to the 11,605 courses with directly measured OSM polygon acreage (\texttt{acreage\_source} $\neq$ \texttt{MICE\_Target}). The observed-only aggregate is \textbf{\$788 billion}, or 82.5\% of the MICE-pooled figure. This result establishes that the bulk of the national opportunity cost is supported by directly measured course geometries; imputation contributes the remaining 17.5\% by extending the estimate to the 28.8\% of courses without OSM polygon coverage.

The total acreage attributable to U.S. golf courses, also pooled across imputations, is approximately 2.30 million acres (Table~\ref{tab:acreage_summary}). Of this, 1.70 million acres (74\%) are in counties classified as urban under the USDA Rural-Urban Continuum Codes, and 598,000 acres (26\%) are in counties classified as rural.

\subsection{Econometric Determinants of Land Value}

To characterize the systematic drivers of opportunity cost, a pooled logarithmic regression was estimated independently in each of the three language stacks. The model regresses the natural log of per-course opportunity cost against the number of holes and a binary indicator for urban county classification:

\begin{equation}
    \ln(V_{OC, i}) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{Holes}_i + \beta_2 \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{Urban}_i] + \varepsilon_i
\end{equation}

The pooled coefficient estimates and standard errors, applying Rubin's Rules independently within each language group ($M = 100$), are reported in Table~\ref{tab:regression_results}.

The cross-language consistency is striking: the intercept ranges from 12.226 (R) to 12.283 (Python), the Holes coefficient ranges from 0.047 (Python) to 0.053 (R), and the Urban indicator ranges from 4.001 (R) to 4.172 (Python). All coefficients are statistically significant at $p < 0.001$ in every implementation. Figure~\ref{fig:forest_plot} visualizes the three coefficient sets with 95\% confidence intervals.

The Holes coefficient ($\beta_1 \approx 0.05$) indicates that each additional hole is associated with approximately a 5\% increase in log opportunity cost, consistent with larger courses concentrating in higher-value geographic areas. The Urban indicator ($\beta_2 \approx 4.0$--$4.2$) implies that, holding course size constant, urban courses are valued at approximately $\exp(4.1) \approx 60$ times rural courses on a per-acre basis.

This 60$\times$ ratio should be interpreted with care. It is partially a consequence of the hybrid valuation algorithm itself: urban courses are assigned the FHFA residential price per acre, and rural courses are assigned the USDA agricultural price per acre, and the FHFA-USDA per-acre ratio at the county level is itself approximately of this magnitude in many markets. The regression coefficient is therefore best understood as a \textit{decomposition} of the bifurcated baseline value rather than as a causal estimate of how a given course's opportunity cost would change if it were relocated from a rural to an urban county. The economic interpretation is that the urban premium dominates all other course-level covariates in the model, with $R^2$ values of 0.70 (R) to 0.77 (Python).

\subsubsection{Synthesis of the Hawaii Findings}

The Hawaii validation produces three results that materially qualify the national estimate. First, the model-to-assessed ratio of 1.33$\times$ provides an empirical anchor for the gap between gross HBU and going-concern current value, with the gap narrower in urban markets where the FHFA proxy performs better. Second, the aggregate Oahu opportunity cost of \$28.6 billion is consistent with the national-scale Honolulu County estimate (\$29.7B) and with Hawaii's appearance in the top-five state list, lending credibility to the national pipeline's geographic resolution. Third, the 81.7\% Preservation/Federal share establishes that the unrestricted gross HBU framework substantially overstates the realizable opportunity cost in this market: the bulk of Oahu's golf land is, under current zoning, not eligible for the residential redeployment that the model assumes. The economic potential is real, the magnitude is large, but the share of that potential that is unlocked by current law is small.

\section{Discussion and Policy Implications}

The empirical results presented in Section 5 produce a layered picture. At the national scale, the aggregate gross HBU opportunity cost of U.S. golf course land is approximately \$944 billion, with an observed-only floor of \$788 billion. At the Honolulu County scale, this figure decomposes into \$28.6 billion of theoretical opportunity cost, of which only roughly \$1.3 billion sits in zones where redevelopment to housing is currently legally permissible. This section interprets these findings in light of the literature reviewed in Section 2, examines the institutional architecture that prevents market clearing, and addresses the methodological limitations that bound the analytical claims.

\subsection{The Magnitude of Spatial Misallocation}

The \$944 billion figure represents the unrealized economic potential of approximately 2.30 million acres of recreational land in the United States. By comparison, this footprint is substantially smaller than the area required to support utility-scale renewable energy infrastructure at decarbonization scale, and is roughly 50 times the land that would be required to construct one million high-density housing units at typical urban densities \parencite{Weinand2025}. The opportunity cost is concentrated geographically in the same housing markets that \textcite{Glaeser2005}, \textcite{Davis2021}, and \textcite{Gyourko2021} identify as the most regulatory-constrained --- coastal California, the New York metropolitan region, southern Florida, and Honolulu County. This co-location is not coincidental. The same regulatory architecture that bids up residential land prices through restrictive zoning is also the architecture that makes redevelopment of existing recreational land politically and legally difficult, producing the paradox that opportunity cost is highest precisely where it is least likely to be realized.

The Lorenz curve of per-course opportunity cost (Figure~\ref{fig:lorenz}) reinforces this point. The aggregate \$944 billion figure is dominated by a relatively small subset of high-value urban courses; the median course contributes a fraction of one percent of the total. This distributional asymmetry implies that policy interventions targeting the highest-cost courses --- particularly in coastal California and the urban Northeast --- could capture a disproportionate share of the welfare gains without requiring nationwide regulatory change.

\subsection{Coasian Frictions and the Allocation of Rights}

The persistence of golf course land use in markets where the residential value of the land vastly exceeds its recreational value is, on a naive Coasian reading, a puzzle. With sufficient gains from trade, parties should bargain to the efficient allocation. The actual outcome --- decades of persistence at low-density recreational use despite escalating opportunity costs --- requires an explanation grounded in transaction costs and rights structure.

The \textcite{Coase1960} framework is most often invoked to argue that initial rights assignments are irrelevant under zero transaction costs. The empirically relevant case, however, is positive transaction costs, in which the initial rights assignment determines the equilibrium allocation. The current legal architecture grants a powerful set of veto rights over redevelopment to incumbent homeowners and golf course members, exercised through three distinct channels: (i) municipal zoning ordinances that prohibit high-density redevelopment of recreationally classified land \parencite{Fischel2015}; (ii) restrictive private covenants that mandate the preservation of golf course use in perpetuity, modifiable only by supermajority of beneficiaries \parencite{Ellickson2022}; and (iii) the political capitalization of the open-space amenity into surrounding property values, which mobilizes intense local opposition to any redevelopment proposal \parencite{Cheshire2002}.

The cumulative effect of these veto channels is that the bargaining surface required for a Coasian transaction is enormous. \textcite{Korngold2024} proposes that state legislatures address this by voiding stale single-family-equivalent covenants under public policy doctrine, but cautions that such voiding may itself constitute a regulatory taking under Fifth Amendment doctrine, requiring just compensation. The economic question this raises is sharp: at \$944 billion in aggregate opportunity cost and roughly \$25 billion locked in Honolulu County's zoned-permissible portion alone, what compensation regime would render the rights reassignment efficient? The literature does not yet offer a confident answer.

\subsection{The Validation Gap: What 1.33\texorpdfstring{$\times$}{x} Tells Us}

The Phase 5a model-to-assessed ratio of 1.33$\times$ is, taken in isolation, a small-sample finding (six courses, all in Hawaii). But the structure of the ratio across the sample is informative. The gap is narrowest for urban Honolulu courses (1.16--1.23$\times$) and widest for rural Big Island courses (1.49--1.69$\times$). This pattern is consistent with two complementary interpretations.

First, the FHFA county-level residential land price index is a more accurate proxy for what assessors are doing in urban markets, where assessors themselves are more likely to use comparable-sales appraisal methods anchored in nearby residential transactions. In rural markets, assessors more often rely on use-value or income-based methods, which produce systematically lower assessments for golf properties classified as recreational, widening the gap between model HBU and assessed value.

Second, the empirical gap is itself a useful estimate of the economic content of the legally-permissible HBU correction. A model-to-assessed ratio of 1.33$\times$ implies that, on average, the gross HBU estimate exceeds going-concern current-use value by approximately one-third. Applied as a uniform deflator to the national \$944B figure, this would yield a net opportunity cost of approximately \$710B --- between the observed-only floor of \$788B (which is itself a gross HBU lower bound) and the unadjusted \$944B Grand Mean. This calibration is suggestive rather than dispositive; the Hawaii sample is small, geographically concentrated, and may not generalize to mainland markets where the assessment regime differs. But it is the first such anchoring for an HBU-based national estimate of this kind, and it provides a defensible basis for interpreting the gross figure as an upper bound on net opportunity cost rather than as a point estimate of net realizable value.

\subsection{The Zoning-Bounded HBU}

The most consequential finding for policy interpretation is the Preservation Paradox documented in Figure~\ref{fig:preservation_paradox}: 81.7\% of Oahu golf land sits in Preservation or Federal/Military zones, where residential redevelopment is statutorily prohibited. Translating the Oahu zoning composition into the legally-permissible HBU framework defined in Section 3.3, the realizable opportunity cost on Oahu under current zoning is approximately \$1.3 billion --- 4.5\% of the gross \$28.6 billion estimate.

This is not a minor caveat. It is the central tension of the policy interpretation. The economic case for redeveloping golf course land in housing-constrained markets is large, but that case applies only to land that current zoning permits to be redeveloped. For the bulk of Oahu's golf footprint, unlocking the gross HBU value would require not just willing buyers and sellers but state-level Land Use Commission boundary amendments or federal land disposal --- processes that operate on timescales of years to decades, with substantial political and constitutional friction.

A natural extension of this analysis would replicate the Phase 5b zoning intersection across other high-value markets where the Hawaii pattern is plausible. If the same Preservation/Restricted-zoning pattern holds for golf land in coastal California, southern Florida, or the New York metro --- and there is strong prior reason to expect that it does, given the historical use of golf courses as anchors for environmentally restrictive master-planned communities --- then the realizable national opportunity cost may be substantially smaller than the \$944 billion gross figure suggests. Quantifying that difference is among the most important directions for follow-on work.

\subsection{Methodological Limitations}

Three limitations are sufficiently important to warrant explicit treatment.

\textit{Marginal versus bulk valuation.} The FHFA county-level residential land price reflects the per-acre price of finished, subdivided residential lots --- land already entitled, served by infrastructure, and ready for construction. Applying this per-acre price to the full undeveloped acreage of a golf course over-states the value a developer could realize from a bulk acquisition, because the bulk parcel must be subdivided, infrastructure must be installed, and lots must be absorbed over a multi-year timeline. Standard appraisal practice applies a subdivision development discount in this context, ranging from approximately 25\% to 60\% of gross retail lot value depending on market conditions and absorption assumptions \parencite{AppraisalInstitute2020}. The \$944 billion gross HBU figure should therefore be interpreted as an upper bound on bulk-liquidation value; a 35\% bulk discount applied uniformly would yield approximately \$614 billion as a more defensible bulk-acquisition estimate, with the 1.33$\times$ Hawaii adjustment producing further downward revision when applied as a net-of-current-use correction.

\textit{V\textsubscript{Current} measurement.} As discussed in Section 3.2.1, the national-scale data sources do not provide course-level capitalized recreational income, so $V_{Current}$ cannot be computed directly for each parcel. The Hawaii pilot validation provides one anchoring strategy via the 1.33$\times$ ratio; alternatives include capitalizing industry-average golf course operating income at market cap rates, or extracting course-level transaction values from the National Golf Foundation's transaction database. None of these alternatives was implemented in the present study, and each would carry its own measurement assumptions.

\textit{Amenity capitalization and adjacent property externalities.} \textcite{Limehouse2012} and \textcite{Cho2009} document that golf courses generate capitalized amenity premiums for adjacent residential properties, and that these premiums (while declining over time) are economically meaningful. Redeveloping a golf course destroys these premiums, imposing a real welfare cost on incumbent homeowners that is not netted out in the present analysis. The unrestricted HBU figure should therefore be understood as a measure of the gross spatial misallocation, not as a measure of the net welfare gain from redevelopment, which would require subtracting the capitalized amenity loss to neighbors. \textcite{Cho2009}'s finding that the amenity premium has declined and become substitutable with other forms of open space suggests that this capitalized loss is a small share of the gross opportunity cost in most markets, but quantifying it directly is an important extension.

\subsection{Renewable Energy as an Alternative Counterfactual}

Given the severe legal frictions associated with residential redevelopment, policymakers might reasonably consider non-residential HBU counterfactuals where regulatory and political resistance is lower. \textcite{Weinand2025} identify utility-scale renewable energy infrastructure --- particularly photovoltaic solar --- as a synergistic alternative use for golf course land. The land footprint of U.S. golf courses is approximately 2.30 million acres, while the total land currently dedicated to utility-scale solar in the U.S. is roughly 5.0 million acres (Figure~\ref{fig:counterfactual_area}). Repurposing even a small fraction of underutilized golf land for solar generation would address the global ``land squeeze'' on renewable energy without triggering the density and traffic concerns that mobilize NIMBY opposition to housing.

The present study did not implement this extension, but the dataset and pipeline are structured to support it.

\section{Conclusion}

This thesis quantifies the aggregate opportunity cost of U.S. golf course land at two scales. At the national scale, applying the appraisal principle of Highest and Best Use to a 16,297-course spatial dataset, with 28.8\% missing acreage resolved through Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations ($M = 100$ per language across three independent computational stacks) and pooled via Rubin's Rules, the gross HBU opportunity cost is approximately \$944 billion. The observed-only floor, computed without imputation on the 11,605 courses with directly measured OSM polygon acreage, is \$788 billion. Spatial concentration is extreme: California, Florida, New York, Texas, and Hawaii together account for over half of the aggregate, and the urban-versus-rural per-acre ratio is approximately 60:1 in the pooled regression decomposition.

At the Honolulu County scale, integration with parcel-level cadastral and zoning data produces three results that materially qualify the national estimate. First, a pilot comparison of model HBU estimates against official tax assessments yields a model-to-assessed ratio of 1.33$\times$, providing the first empirical anchor for the gap between gross HBU and going-concern current-use value in this literature. Second, the aggregate Oahu opportunity cost is \$28.6 billion across 1,072 unique TMK parcels, consistent with the national pipeline's Honolulu County estimate. Third --- and most consequentially --- 81.7\% of Oahu's golf footprint sits in Preservation or Federal/Military zones where residential redevelopment is currently statutorily prohibited, leaving only roughly \$1.3 billion of the gross estimate as legally realizable under the existing zoning regime.

These two findings are complementary, not contradictory. The economic potential of U.S. golf course land is large, geographically concentrated, and empirically tractable; the validation against Honolulu municipal records lends credibility to the national-scale geographic structure. But the realizability of that potential is bounded by a layered regulatory architecture --- public zoning, private covenants, and capitalized adjacent-property amenity values --- that the HBU framework alone cannot dissolve. Unlocking even a meaningful fraction of the locked value would require simultaneous reform on multiple legal fronts, including Land Use Commission boundary amendments, voiding of stale private covenants under public policy doctrine, and the resolution of associated Fifth Amendment takings claims.

Three directions for future research follow naturally from these findings. First, the Phase 5b zoning intersection methodology should be replicated in other high-value coastal markets --- particularly the Bay Area, Los Angeles, southern Florida, and the New York metro --- to test whether the Preservation Paradox documented on Oahu generalizes, and to produce a national legally-permissible HBU estimate alongside the unrestricted figure. Second, $V_{Current}$ should be measured directly using transaction data from the National Golf Foundation or income-capitalization methods, refining the 1.33$\times$ Hawaii anchor into a market-specific net opportunity cost. Third, $V_{Renewable}$ should be integrated formally into the HBU framework as a third counterfactual, allowing the analysis to identify parcels where solar or wind generation produces higher residual value than residential redevelopment. The present thesis offers the dataset, the pipeline, and the validation logic to support all three extensions.

The \$944 billion figure is not a forecast of how much land value will be unlocked, nor a recommendation that every golf course in the country be converted to housing or renewables. It is a measurement: a quantification of the spatial misallocation embedded in the current allocation of recreational land in the United States. What policy makes of that measurement is a separate question, but the measurement itself --- bounded, validated, and reproduced across three computational stacks --- is the contribution this thesis offers to the literature on land use, housing supply, and regulatory economics.
