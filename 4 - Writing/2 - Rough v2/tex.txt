\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Hook Me There and Now}
A kind and patient professor once told me that the basis of many research topics are that of anecdotal concepts turned evidenced based given the right data and methodology. This is the basis of my thesis, as for me I have been fascinated with how the golf land use has existed within that of the State of Hawai'i. For this, it started when I was doing surface level research and noticed that several golf courses had been partially, if not fully, placed on P-2 zoned land, which in Hawai'i is zoned as Preservation Land use. To this, it as well benefits the courses as they have little to no tax burden that should be had by owning and opperating a business on the stated land that they are upon, or zoned in. This is a very interesting concept to me, as it is a very clear example of how land use can be misallocated in a way that benefits a select few, while the rest of the population suffers from the opportunity cost of not being able to use that land for more productive purposes. This has led me to wonder about the broader implications of golf courses and their impact on urban land use, especially in suburban areas where land is at a premium.

\subsection{Specific Focus}
I am aiming at, with the use of this paper and attached research, to examine the total value impacted by the existence of golf courses in the United States, and how much of that value is lost or gained due to the opportunity cost of not being able to use that land for other purposes. This is a very important topic, as it has implications for urban planning, zoning laws, and the overall economic efficiency of land use in the United States. By quantifying the opportunity cost of golf courses, I hope to provide a clearer picture of the true cost of maintaining these recreational spaces and to inform policy decisions regarding land use and zoning. To this effect, I expect that golf courses will largely have higher productivity in more rural areas while in more urban areas, the opportunity cost will be much higher due to the scarcity of land and the potential for more productive uses such as housing or commercial development.

\subsection{The Gap from Literature and Policy}
From my anecdotal research, and experiences, \cite{AppraisalInstitute2020}, I know of the stated points of Highest and Best Use, or refered to as HBU: Legally Permissible, Physically Possible, Financially Feasible, and Maximally Productive. Legally permissible being that of how under any given law within a region, county or area, the improvement, building, must be that of a  permissible use, such as a Residential Improvement, such as a traditional detatched single family residence not being allowed for recent construction in a zone designated for pure business use. Physically possible being that of how the land must be able to support the improvement, sturcturally, and in terms of access, such as a landlocked parcel not being able to be developed for residential use. Financially feasible being that of how the improvement must be able to generate enough income to justify the cost of development, such as a commercial building needing to have enough potential tenants or customers to cover the costs of construction and operation. Maximally productive being that of how the improvement must be able to generate the highest possible return on investment, such as a high-rise office building being more productive than a single-story retail space on the same parcel of land.

With regards to that of \cite{AppraisalInstitute2020}, I also know that there are also two statments of counterfactual to the evidence based appraisal factors. This would be the use of Hypothetical Condition, which is usually used in the event of attempting to assess the possible negative impact of a given related disaster, such as an oil spill on a property. Then there is the use of an Extraordinary Assumption, which is a given assumption that is usually made for the sake of analysis for convience, such as if an interior wall is not visible due to the presence of a large piece of furniture, the appraiser may make an extraordinary assumption about the condition of that wall based on the visible portion and the overall condition of the property.

With regards to this paper I will primarily be implimenting that of Hypothetical Conditions, such that what if all zoning for all lands for golf courses were on to be changed to enable teh Maximally Productive use of the land, and what would be the opportunity cost of maintaining the land as golf courses. This is a very important question, as it has implications for urban planning, zoning laws, and the overall economic efficiency of land use in the United States. By quantifying the opportunity cost of golf courses, I hope to provide a clearer picture of the true cost of maintaining these recreational spaces and to inform policy decisions regarding land use and zoning.
